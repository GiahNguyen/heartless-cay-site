The island's most contested resource.

What They Are

Crystals are Briar's most valuable natural resource and its most politically contested one. They form naturally inside the island's blue obsidian deposits and cannot be successfully replicated or sustained beyond Briar's borders. Every attempt to export a living crystal has ended the same way, the crystal goes dark within days of leaving the island's soil.

What reaches the human world is essentially a dying thing, still functional for a time, but cut off from the source that made it what it is.

How They Work

Crystals are not a fixed resource. They respond to their environment, to proximity to obsidian, to depth underground, to the supernatural energy density of their surroundings. The same crystal behaves differently in different parts of Briar. Separated from their natural context, they lose their responsive quality entirely. What humans study in laboratories is a crystal performing a fraction of what it actually is.

More significantly, crystals respond to people. To emotion. To intention. To supernaturals. Human scientists keep getting anomalous results because the crystal is responding to the researcher, not performing a fixed function.

Light and Location

  • Brimson · The Heartless Cay · St. Raphael's Blue-white, steady, sourceless. Cut and mounted in iron brackets. The crystal equivalent of fluorescent lighting.
  • The Beowulf Cavern Blue at the passages, warm amber in the depths. The closest thing the pack has to a natural sunrise.
  • The Enchanted Forest Floating, translucent, diamond-shaped. Casting light that does not behave the way light should. The most alive crystals on the island.
  • Ashfalls Factory Sector Amber-grey, dim, worn down. The workers call this bleeding the crystals — running them past their natural output until they go dark.
  • Mount Azazel The oldest and most powerful formations. Nobody fully understands why they behave the way they do.

Who Controls Them

The Beowulf mine the crystals. The NightWing broker them. Ashfalls processes them. The human world buys them. The factory workers breathe the dust and receive none of the profit.

This arrangement is the economic core of Briar's generational conflict — not ideology, but money, control, and the indignation of watching something you built being used for something you never agreed to.

What the human world calls a power source, Briar calls a heart.
